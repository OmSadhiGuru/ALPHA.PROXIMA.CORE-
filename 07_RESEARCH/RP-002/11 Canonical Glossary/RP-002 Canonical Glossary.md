# RP-002 — Canonical Glossary

All definitions are canonical for Alpha Proxima purposes and are derived from the reviewed source set (DOC-A, DOC-B). Evidence classifications reference the [[12 Evidence Registry/RP-002 Evidence Registry]].

---

## A

**Adult Neurogenesis**
The production of new neurons in the adult brain, specifically in the dentate gyrus (DG) subgranular zone (SGZ) of the hippocampus. New granule cells contribute to pattern separation capacity and cognitive flexibility. Well-established in rodents; rate and functional significance in adult humans remain contested. [E-003]

**AMPA Receptor**
A type of ionotropic glutamate receptor. During LTP, AMPA receptors are inserted into the postsynaptic membrane (increasing synaptic strength) or removed during LTD. Synaptic homeostasis is implemented via scaling of AMPA receptor surface expression across all synapses on a neuron. [C-002]

**Astrocyte**
A type of glial cell that participates actively in synaptic plasticity via the tripartite synapse model. Functions: glutamate clearance (preventing excitotoxicity), release of D-serine (NMDA co-agonist enabling LTP), glycogen-lactate energy supply to active neurons. [E-001]

---

## B

**Basolateral Amygdala (BLA)**
A subcortical structure that modulates memory encoding strength in proportion to emotional arousal. Mechanism: HPA stress axis → noradrenergic release → BLA activation → amplified hippocampal LTP for emotionally salient events. Explains flashbulb memories and traumatic memory formation. [C-006]

---

## C

**CaMKII (Calcium/Calmodulin-dependent Protein Kinase II)**
A key molecular switch in LTP induction. Activated by calcium influx through NMDA receptors; phosphorylates AMPA receptors and initiates downstream protein synthesis cascades. [C-002]

**Collective Memory**
(Halbwachs, 1925) Memory that is socially constituted — the shared stock of representations about the past held by a community, constituted through social frameworks, rituals, and institutions. Irreducible to the sum of individual memories. Cross-disciplinary implication: Alpha Proxima's institutional memory is a collective memory artifact. [DOC-B]

**Complementary Learning Systems (CLS) Theory**
A computational theory proposing that the hippocampus and neocortex perform complementary functions: the hippocampus rapidly encodes individual episodes (pattern separation, sparse coding); the neocortex slowly extracts statistical regularities across episodes (overlapping representations, generalization). Integration occurs via sleep replay. [M-004]

**Consolidation**
The process by which newly encoded memories are stabilized over time. Operates at two levels: synaptic consolidation (minutes-hours; LTP, protein synthesis) and systemic consolidation (weeks-years; hippocampal replay → neocortical transfer). [C-004]

---

## D

**Declarative Memory**
Memory that is available to conscious recollection, including episodic memory (autobiographical events) and semantic memory (facts, concepts). Requires hippocampal formation for acquisition; contrasts with non-declarative (implicit, procedural) memory.

**Dentate Gyrus (DG)**
A subfield of the hippocampal formation, the input gateway of the trisynaptic circuit. Primary function: pattern separation — orthogonalizing similar inputs to minimize interference between overlapping memories. Adult neurogenesis occurs in the DG subgranular zone. [C-007]

**Dendritic Spine**
Small protrusions on dendrites that are the primary sites of excitatory synapses. Their morphology (size, shape, density) changes with LTP/LTD and represents the structural substrate of synaptic memory. Spine morphogenesis is one of the four neuroplasticity mechanisms.

---

## E

**Encoding**
The initial process by which external information is transformed into a neural representation. Encoding strength is modulated by attention (dlPFC), emotional salience (BLA), and prior knowledge (mPFC schemas). Context during encoding becomes a retrieval cue (encoding specificity principle). [C-010]

**Engram**
The physical trace left in the brain by a specific experience — the neural substrate of a specific memory. Engram cells have been identified via optogenetics; the code by which they represent memory content remains unknown. [Q-001]

**Entorhinal Cortex (EC)**
The primary gateway between the neocortex and hippocampus in the trisynaptic circuit. Both the entry point (perforant path → DG) and exit point (CA1 → EC) for hippocampal memory processing. Grid cells (spatial navigation) are found in the medial EC.

**Episodic Memory**
Memory for specific autobiographical events — "what happened, where, and when." Distinguished from semantic memory by its spatiotemporal context. Episodic memory may permanently require hippocampal involvement (MTT position) or may transfer to hippocampus-independent storage (SCT position). [M-001]

---

## G

**Gamma Rhythm**
High-frequency neural oscillations (30-100 Hz) generated in cortical and hippocampal circuits. During online encoding, gamma cycles are nested within theta rhythm (theta-gamma coupling), potentially encoding individual working memory items within each gamma packet. [E-005]

**Glial Coupling**
The active participation of glial cells (astrocytes and microglia) in synaptic plasticity and memory consolidation. The tripartite synapse model extends the classical two-cell (pre/postsynaptic neuron) model to include the perisynaptic astrocytic process as a functional third element. [E-001]

---

## H

**Hebbian Plasticity**
Synaptic strengthening that occurs when presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons are repeatedly co-active ("neurons that fire together, wire together"). The biological implementation is LTP/LTD via NMDA receptor activation. Named for Donald Hebb (1949). [C-002]

**Hippocampal Formation**
The hippocampal subfields (CA1, CA3, DG) together with the subiculum, forming the core of the medial temporal lobe memory system. Required for formation of new declarative memories [C-003]; its role in maintaining old memories is contested [M-001].

---

## L

**Long-Term Depression (LTD)**
Activity-dependent weakening of synaptic connections — the complement of LTP. Involves AMPA receptor removal from the postsynaptic membrane. Functions in memory erasure, forgetting, and synaptic specificity.

**Long-Term Potentiation (LTP)**
Activity-dependent strengthening of synaptic connections — the primary cellular mechanism of memory formation [C-002]. Triggered by coincident pre- and postsynaptic activity → NMDA receptor activation → calcium influx → CaMKII → AMPA receptor insertion + protein synthesis.

---

## M

**Microglia**
Resident immune cells of the brain that perform synaptic pruning — eliminating weak or inappropriate synaptic connections during slow-wave sleep. Part of the tripartite synapse / glial coupling framework. [E-001]

**mTOR Pathway**
Mammalian target of rapamycin — a kinase pathway activated during LTP that initiates local protein synthesis at active synapses, required for late-phase LTP and long-term memory consolidation. [C-002]

---

## N

**NMDA Receptor**
A glutamate receptor that requires simultaneous presynaptic glutamate release AND postsynaptic depolarization to open (a "coincidence detector"). NMDA activation is the trigger for LTP induction and for the reconsolidation window during memory retrieval. [C-002, C-008]

---

## P

**Parametric Memory (AI)**
Knowledge encoded in the weights of a neural network through training. Stable and fast to access at inference, but cannot be updated without retraining. The long-term semantic memory analogue in AI systems. [DOC-B, Initiative D]

**Pattern Completion**
The ability to reconstruct a full memory representation from a partial cue. Implemented in CA3 via extensive recurrent collaterals (autoassociative network). Enables recognition from partial inputs; risk: false pattern completion (intrusion errors). [C-007]

**Pattern Separation**
The ability to distinguish between similar inputs by creating distinct, non-overlapping neural representations. Implemented in the dentate gyrus via sparse coding and adult neurogenesis. Prevents interference between similar memories. [C-007]

**Perineuronal Net (PNN)**
Extracellular matrix scaffolding around certain neurons, proposed as a non-synaptic structural substrate that may stabilize synaptic configurations over long timescales, potentially helping resolve the protein turnover paradox. [E-002]

**PKMζ (Protein Kinase Mζ)**
An atypical protein kinase C isoform proposed as the molecular "memory maintenance" protein — constitutively active, continuously maintains synaptic potentiation. ZIP (a PKMζ inhibitor) erases established long-term memories when injected into brain regions. Its role in the reconsolidation restabilization step is central. [C-008]

---

## R

**Reconsolidation**
The process by which a retrieved memory re-enters a labile, unstable state and must be actively restabilized through protein synthesis to persist. Defines a post-retrieval window (~6 hours) during which the memory can be modified. [C-008]

**Retrieval**
The process of accessing and reconstructing a stored memory. Retrieval is not passive readout — it re-creates the memory (reconstructive memory [C-001]) and opens the reconsolidation window [C-008]. Encoding context shapes retrieval success (encoding specificity [C-010]).

---

## S

**Semantic Memory**
Factual, decontextualized knowledge without spatiotemporal encoding context (e.g., knowing that Paris is the capital of France, without remembering when or where you learned it). Distinguished from episodic memory by Tulving (1972).

**Sharp-Wave Ripple (SWR)**
High-frequency oscillatory bursts (150-250 Hz) originating in CA3 and propagating through CA1 and to the neocortex. Generated during NREM sleep and quiet waking. The vehicle for memory replay and systemic consolidation — during SWR events, recently encoded sequences are replayed at compressed timescale, driving neocortical plasticity. [C-005]

**Sleep Spindle**
Thalamic oscillations (11-16 Hz) occurring during NREM sleep. Participate in memory consolidation coordinated with SWR and cortical slow oscillations (delta). [C-005]

**Subiculum**
Output subfield of the hippocampal formation, receiving projections from CA1 and projecting to the entorhinal cortex and other targets. The primary output stage of the trisynaptic circuit.

**Synaptic Homeostasis**
Scaling of synaptic strength across all synapses on a neuron (as opposed to Hebbian LTP, which is synapse-specific). During sleep, AMPA receptor surface expression is downscaled globally, maintaining the neuron's dynamic range and preventing saturation. [DOC-A]

---

## T

**Theta Rhythm**
Low-frequency oscillations (4-8 Hz) generated in the hippocampus and entorhinal cortex. Organizes the temporal structure of online encoding; gamma cycles are nested within theta cycles (theta-gamma coupling). Also prominent during REM sleep, where it may contribute to memory consolidation. [E-005]

**Transient Memory (AI)**
Context windows and hidden states in AI systems — session-scoped, capacity-limited, lost at reset. The working memory analogue in AI systems. [DOC-B, Initiative D]

**Tripartite Synapse**
Extended synapse model including three components: presynaptic neuron, postsynaptic neuron, and perisynaptic astrocyte process. The astrocyte participates actively in neurotransmitter clearance, co-agonist release, and energy supply. [E-001]

**Trisynaptic Circuit**
The canonical hippocampal circuit: Entorhinal Cortex → Dentate Gyrus (perforant path) → CA3 (mossy fibers) → CA1 (Schaffer collaterals) → Subiculum → Entorhinal Cortex. Pattern separation (DG) and pattern completion (CA3) are computationally distinct operations in this circuit. [C-007]

---

## U

**Ubiquitin-Proteasome Pathway**
Protein degradation system activated during memory reconsolidation. NMDA activation during retrieval triggers ubiquitin-proteasome degradation of stabilizing synaptic proteins, creating the labile reconsolidation window. New protein synthesis (PKMζ, etc.) then restabilizes the modified trace. [C-008]

---

## W

**Working Memory**
A temporary, capacity-limited system for actively maintaining and manipulating information. Implemented by dlPFC and frontoparietal control network (FPCN). Classical model (Baddeley): phonological loop + visuospatial sketchpad + central executive. Capacity: ~7 ± 2 items; possibly ~4 chunks in modern accounts. [C-009]
