# Concept Note — Trauma Memory

---

## Canonical Definition

**Trauma memory** refers to the distinctive encoding and retrieval characteristics of memories formed under conditions of extreme stress or threat. The defining clinical expression is PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder), in which traumatic memories are experienced as intrusive, involuntary, sensory-dominant, and phenomenologically present-tense — qualitatively different from ordinary narrative recall [P-002].

---

## Neuroscience of Trauma Encoding

Under extreme threat, the HPA axis activates, flooding the hippocampus and amygdala with stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline) and noradrenaline. This creates:

| Effect | Mechanism | Memory Consequence |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| Hyperstrong encoding | BLA amplification via noradrenergic pathway [C-006] | Emotionally salient details encoded very strongly |
| Fragmented narrative structure | Hippocampal function suppressed under extreme cortisol | Context and temporal sequence poorly encoded |
| Sensory dominance | Sensory cortex encoding proceeds relatively normally | Sensory fragments (smells, sounds) encode strongly without narrative frame |
| Reconsolidation disruption | Stress hormones disrupt normal reconsolidation | Retrieved trauma memories may fail to restabilize normally → intrusions persist |

---

## PTSD as Memory Architecture Failure

PTSD can be understood as a disruption of the normal reconstructive memory architecture:
- Normal memories: retrieved, updated with current context, reconsolidated in narrative form
- Traumatic memories (PTSD): retrieved involuntarily (intrusions), experienced as present-tense, resist narrative integration, reconsolidation window disrupted by hyperactivated stress response

The trauma memory is structurally "stuck" — it lacks the contextual framing (hippocampal encoding) that marks ordinary memories as past events.

---

## Therapeutic Approaches

| Approach | Mechanism | Evidence |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Prolonged Exposure | Extinction of conditioned fear via non-reinforced CS presentation | Strong RCT evidence; targets fear learning, not memory trace itself |
| EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization) | Dual attention during trauma recall; mechanism disputed | Strong RCT evidence; mechanism unclear |
| Reconsolidation-Based Therapy | Reactivate trauma memory → open window → allow reconsolidation with reduced physiological response | Promising; not yet standardized clinical protocol [Q-004] |
| Propranolol (beta blocker) | Beta-adrenergic blockade during reconsolidation window | Reduces physiological fear responding; does not erase memory narrative |

---

## Phenomenology [P-002]

First-person accounts of traumatic memory are distinct from ordinary recall:
- Involuntary intrusion (not sought; cannot be suppressed)
- Sensory vividness without narrative context
- Present-tense experience ("it's happening now")
- Emotional and physiological re-experiencing (flashback)
- Fragmentation — pieces, not stories

This phenomenology is itself evidence for the neuroscience model: sensory without narrative = amygdala/sensory cortex without hippocampal context.
