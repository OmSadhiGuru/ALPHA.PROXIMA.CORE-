# RP-001 — Canonical Glossary

---

## Purpose

This glossary establishes the canonical definitions for all technical terms used in RP-001 research notes. When a term appears in a research note, it is defined here — not redefined per note. This ensures consistency across the Atlas.

Per [[Book III - Knowledge Integrity]], Art. V, Sec. 5.1: methodological transparency requires that all claims be evaluable. Terms that are undefined or used inconsistently undermine this. This glossary is an epistemic requirement, not merely a reference tool.

---

## How Definitions Work Here

Each entry includes:
- **Term** (with variant spellings or synonyms noted)
- **Definition** as used within RP-001
- **Source / tradition** — where the definition originates
- **Contested?** — whether the definition itself is disputed
- **Evidence class** of the concept being defined (not the definition itself)

When a term is used differently by different frameworks, all major usages are documented. We do not pick one and suppress the others.

## Core Terms

### Access Consciousness
**Definition:** A mental state is access-conscious if it is available for use in reasoning, reporting, and the direct control of action.  
**Coined by:** Ned Block (1995)  
**Contested?** The distinction between access and phenomenal consciousness is disputed; some argue they cannot be cleanly separated  
**Evidence class:** M — the distinction itself is contested  
**See also:** Phenomenal Consciousness; Global Workspace Theory

---

### Attention
**Definition:** The selective prioritisation of certain information for further processing, at the expense of other information.  
**Note:** Attention and consciousness are often conflated but are dissociable. It is possible to attend to something without it being conscious, and for something to be conscious without deliberate attention.  
**Evidence class:** C for the existence of selective attention; M for the relationship between attention and consciousness

---

### Awareness
**Definition (scientific):** The state of being conscious of something; often used interchangeably with consciousness in lay usage but technically distinguished in some frameworks.  
**Definition (contemplative):** In many contemplative traditions, "awareness" refers specifically to the background capacity for experience — the "witness" or "knowing quality" — distinct from the contents of experience. In Advaita and Dzogchen, awareness is considered primary.  
**Contested?** Yes — the relationship between awareness, attention, and consciousness is actively debated  
**Evidence class:** Q — how awareness, attention, and consciousness relate remains an open question

---

### Binding
**Definition:** The process by which disparate features of experience (colour, shape, motion, sound) are unified into a single coherent percept.  
**Significance:** How the brain achieves binding is an unsolved problem — the "binding problem." Different frameworks propose different solutions (gamma oscillations, global workspace broadcast, IIT's integration).  
**Evidence class:** C that binding occurs; M for mechanisms; Q for the deepest explanation  
**See also:** Unity of Consciousness

---

### Consciousness
**Definition (functional):** The state of being aware of and responsive to one's surroundings.  
**Definition (phenomenal):** The subjective, experiential dimension of mental life — "what it is like" to be in a particular state.  
**Note:** These two senses correspond to access consciousness and phenomenal consciousness respectively (Block). Most empirical research addresses functional consciousness; the phenomenal dimension is the subject of the hard problem.  
**Evidence class:** C that consciousness in the functional sense exists; Q for its ultimate nature; P for first-person investigation of its phenomenal character

---

### Default Mode Network (DMN)
**Definition:** A network of brain regions (medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex, angular gyrus, and others) that is active during rest, self-referential thought, mind-wandering, and social cognition. Typically deactivated during externally-focused tasks.  
**Relevance to consciousness:** The DMN is associated with self-referential processing; disruption of DMN activity correlates with altered states (psychedelics, meditation, anaesthesia)  
**Evidence class:** C for DMN anatomy and function; M for its precise role in consciousness and self

---

### Ego / Ego Dissolution
**Definition (psychological):** The ego is the sense of individual selfhood — the experience of being a bounded, continuous "I" separate from the world.  
**Definition (ego dissolution):** A state in which the ordinary boundaries of the self temporarily break down; reported in deep meditation, psychedelic experiences, near-death experiences.  
**Evidence class:** C that ego dissolution is reported across traditions; E for its neural correlates (research is active but not fully replicated); P for the subjective experience

---

### Hard Problem of Consciousness
**Definition:** David Chalmers' (1995) formulation of the question: why are physical processes accompanied by subjective experience? Why is there something it is like to be in a particular brain state, rather than all processing occurring "in the dark"?  
**Status:** Unsolved. No scientific framework has provided a widely accepted solution.  
**Evidence class:** Q — the hard problem is an open question  
**See also:** Explanatory Gap; Qualia

---

### Kosha
**Definition (Sanskrit, yogic framework):** Literally "sheath." The Taittiriya Upanishad describes five Koshas (Pancha Kosha) — layers of the self that surround pure consciousness (Atman): Annamaya (physical body), Pranamaya (energy body), Manomaya (mental body), Vijnanamaya (intellectual body), Anandamaya (bliss body).  
**Evidence class:** P — phenomenological framework from the yogic tradition; not generalisable as an objective empirical claim

---

### Neural Correlates of Consciousness (NCCs)
**Definition:** The minimal set of neural mechanisms that are jointly sufficient for a specific conscious percept.  
**Note:** Identifying NCCs is a major goal of empirical consciousness research. NCCs explain *what* is correlated with consciousness; they do not by themselves explain *why* those processes are accompanied by experience.  
**Evidence class:** C that NCCs exist and can be identified for specific percepts; M for the interpretation of what NCCs tell us about the nature of consciousness

---

### Phenomenal Consciousness
**Definition:** The subjective, qualitative character of experience — "what it is like" to see red, feel pain, or smell coffee. Also called "qualia" or the "phenomenal character" of experience.  
**Coined by:** Ned Block (1995), though the concept is older  
**Contested?** Whether phenomenal consciousness is distinct from access consciousness, and whether it is explicable in physical terms, are among the most contested questions in philosophy of mind  
**Evidence class:** Q — the nature of phenomenal consciousness is an open question

---

### Prana / Pranayama
**Definition (Sanskrit, yogic framework):** Prana is the vital life energy said to flow through the body via channels (Nadis). Pranayama is the set of breathing practices that regulate Prana flow.  
**Evidence class:** P — phenomenological framework; some correlates have been studied empirically (effects of specific breathing patterns on autonomic nervous system, HRV, and brain activity) at Class E–M level

---

### Qualia (singular: Quale)
**Definition:** The intrinsic, subjective properties of experience — the "raw feels." The redness of red, the painfulness of pain, the taste of coffee, independent of any functional role these experiences play.  
**Significance:** Qualia are the central exhibit in arguments for the hard problem; if qualia exist and have intrinsic character, they seem to resist physical explanation.  
**Contested?** Yes — eliminativists deny qualia exist (Dennett); functionalists argue qualia reduce to functional properties; dualists argue they are irreducible  
**Evidence class:** Q — the existence and nature of qualia is among philosophy of mind's deepest open questions

---

### Samadhi
**Definition (Sanskrit, yogic/Buddhist framework):** A state of profound meditative absorption in which ordinary subject-object duality is transcended. Yogic traditions distinguish many levels: Savikalpa Samadhi (with thought), Nirvikalpa Samadhi (without thought), Sahaja Samadhi (spontaneous, natural absorption).  
**Evidence class:** P — phenomenological report from contemplative traditions; neural correlates of deep meditative absorption are studied at Class E level

---

### Self-Model / Minimal Self
**Definition:** The brain's model of itself as an agent in the world. The minimal self is the sense of being a subject of experience, distinct from the narrative self (the autobiographical story we tell about ourselves).  
**Theoretical home:** Predictive Processing / Active Inference (Friston); Metzinger's Being No One  
**Evidence class:** M — the self-model theory is a developed framework with empirical implications

---

### Subjective Experience
**Definition:** The first-person, "inside" dimension of mental life — experience as it is felt from the inside, rather than observed from the outside. Synonymous with phenomenal consciousness in most uses.  
**Note:** Subjective experience is definitionally inaccessible to third-person observation; this is why it poses a unique methodological challenge to science.

---

### Unity of Consciousness
**Definition:** The experienced wholeness or integration of consciousness — the way that at any moment, all the contents of experience seem to belong to a single, unified field rather than multiple separate streams.  
**Evidence class:** C that the experience of unity is reported; M for its neural explanation; Q for its ultimate nature
