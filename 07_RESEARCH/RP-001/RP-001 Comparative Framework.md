# RP-001 — Comparative Framework

---

## Purpose

This document is the structured comparison engine for RP-001. When two or more theoretical frameworks make competing claims about the same domain of consciousness, those competing claims are documented here — preserved in their genuine disagreement, not merged into false consensus.

This document is the model for how Alpha Proxima handles intellectual conflict: with clarity, not resolution. The goal is not to pick a winner. The goal is to understand what each framework claims, on what grounds, and where the genuine disagreement lies.

Per [[Book III - Knowledge Integrity]], Art. I, Sec. 1.4: *"When evidence conflicts, the conflict must be preserved. The temptation to merge conflicting theories into artificial consensus — to smooth over genuine intellectual disagreement for the sake of clarity — is explicitly prohibited."*

---

## How to Read This Document

Each comparison entry:
1. States the question where frameworks diverge
2. Summarises each framework's position
3. Identifies the *nature* of the disagreement (empirical, conceptual, or methodological)
4. Assigns evidence classes to each position
5. Identifies what evidence, if available, would resolve the disagreement

## Comparison 1 — Neural Correlates vs. Neural Sufficiency

**Question:** Are neural correlates of consciousness (NCCs) merely correlated with consciousness, or are they sufficient to explain it?

**Position A — GWT / Predictive Processing:** NCCs are sufficient. Consciousness *is* the relevant neural processing. Explaining the processing explains the experience.  
**Evidence class:** M — this is the dominant neuroscientific view but is contested  

**Position B — IIT:** NCCs are not merely correlates; consciousness *is* integrated information (Φ). A system with the right Φ is conscious regardless of substrate. Neural substrates are one implementation, not the explanation.  
**Evidence class:** M — a mathematically developed competing framework  

**Position C — Hard Problem response (Chalmers, Jackson):** No neural account can explain why physical processes are accompanied by subjective experience. NCCs explain the functional correlates of consciousness (what we can report), not the phenomenal character (what it is like). There is an explanatory gap.  
**Evidence class:** Q — open philosophical question; the hard problem has not been solved by any framework  

**Nature of disagreement:** Partly empirical (what do NCCs predict?), largely conceptual (what counts as an explanation of consciousness?)

**What would resolve it:** A theory that either (a) reduces phenomenal experience to functional/physical properties with philosophical coherence, or (b) demonstrates that no such reduction is possible. Neither has occurred.

## Comparison 2 — Global vs. Local: Where Does Consciousness Arise?

**Question:** Does consciousness require global broadcast across the brain, or can it arise locally in sensory cortices?

**Position A — Global Workspace Theory:** Consciousness requires global broadcast. Unconscious processing occurs locally; consciousness is the ignition of a global workspace. Evidence: patients with local lesions lose specific contents of consciousness but not consciousness itself; global ignition events correlate with conscious reports.  
**Evidence class:** M  

**Position B — Recurrent Processing Theory (Lamme):** Consciousness arises from local recurrent processing in sensory cortex. Global broadcast is required for *reportability* but not for phenomenal experience. A patient may have an experience they cannot report. Evidence: some studies show neural correlates of visual experience before global workspace activity.  
**Evidence class:** M  

**Nature of disagreement:** Empirical, but also conceptual — the debate depends on how you distinguish phenomenal consciousness from access consciousness.

**Key empirical test:** Paradigms that dissociate subjective report from neural activity — e.g., inattentional blindness, masking paradigms, split-brain studies.

## Comparison 3 — The Substrate Question: Can Non-Biological Systems Be Conscious?

**Question:** Is consciousness substrate-dependent (requires biological neural tissue) or substrate-independent (any system with the right organisation can be conscious)?

**Position A — Biological Naturalism (Searle):** Consciousness is a biological phenomenon caused by specific neurobiological processes. Digital computers — regardless of their functional organisation — cannot be conscious because they lack the right causal powers. The Chinese Room argument is the canonical statement of this position.  
**Evidence class:** S — philosophically developed; no empirical test available  

**Position B — Functionalism:** What matters is functional organisation, not substrate. Any system that implements the right functional relationships can be conscious. This is the implicit assumption of most computational theories of mind and is necessary for AI consciousness to be possible.  
**Evidence class:** M — the dominant position in cognitive science and philosophy of mind  

**Position C — IIT's position:** Consciousness is tied to Φ — integrated information. Some digital computers (feedforward networks) have low Φ and would be largely unconscious; others might have high Φ. Biological systems tend to have high Φ, but this is not substrate-dependence per se.  
**Evidence class:** M  

**Nature of disagreement:** Philosophical and empirical. No current test can determine whether a non-biological system is conscious; we lack a consciousness meter.

**Relevance to Alpha Proxima:** This question bears directly on whether AI participants (LUMIAION, departments) can have any form of experience. The Foundation's current position: this is a Class Q open question; no claims about AI consciousness are made or implied in any Foundation document.

## Comparison 4 — Scientific vs. Phenomenological: Methodological Conflict

**Question:** Can first-person phenomenological investigation (meditation, introspection) produce reliable knowledge about consciousness?

**Position A — Standard scientific position:** First-person reports are useful as data but cannot be the primary method of investigating consciousness. Reports are subject to introspective error, expectation effects, and cultural influence. Only third-person, replicable, objective methods generate scientific knowledge.  
**Evidence class:** C for the general caution; the limits of introspection are well-documented  

**Position B — Neurophenomenology (Varela, Thompson, Metzinger):** First-person and third-person methods must be integrated. Phenomenological investigation, properly conducted, provides data that third-person methods cannot access. Meditation traditions have developed rigorous first-person methodologies that can complement neuroscience.  
**Evidence class:** M — a developed research program with empirical outputs (Mind and Life Institute)  

**Position C — Contemplative traditions:** The deepest knowledge about consciousness comes from direct investigation through sustained practice. Scientific methods, while valuable, operate at the level of the contents of consciousness, not consciousness itself. The experiential discoveries of meditation science are not reducible to third-person reports.  
**Evidence class:** P — phenomenological; valid within its domain  

**Nature of disagreement:** Methodological. This is not merely a disagreement about findings; it is a disagreement about what counts as valid investigation.

**Alpha Proxima's approach:** The Foundation holds all three positions open (Class M / Q) and records the Founder's phenomenological reports as Class P — valid data within their domain, not generalisable as objective claims without additional evidence. This is the institutional resolution of the methodological conflict: inclusivity with classification, not false consensus.

## Comparison 5 — The Self: Construct or Reality?

**Question:** Is the sense of self a construction of the brain, or does it reflect a genuine, stable entity?

**Position A — Neuroscience / Cognitive Science consensus:** The self is a construction — a narrative generated by the brain, not a stable entity that exists independently. Evidence: split-brain studies, cases of dissociation, predictive processing accounts of self as a model.  
**Evidence class:** C for the basic constructionist claim; M for the specific mechanisms  

**Position B — Buddhist phenomenology:** The self is a construction — specifically, a habitual pattern of identification with mental events that is itself impermanent and without fixed essence (Anatta). Direct investigation reveals no permanent self underlying experience.  
**Evidence class:** P — phenomenological; converges with neuroscience on the constructed nature of self  

**Position C — Non-dual traditions (Advaita, Dzogchen):** The apparent self (ego) is indeed a construction, but there is a deeper "I" — pure awareness — that is not constructed and not impermanent. The dissolution of the constructed self reveals this prior awareness, not nothingness.  
**Evidence class:** P — phenomenological; makes a different claim than Buddhism about what remains after self-deconstruction  

**Nature of disagreement:** Between Positions B and C: whether what remains after ego-dissolution is "no-self" (Buddhism) or "pure awareness / consciousness" (non-dual traditions). This is a genuine phenomenological disagreement among traditions, not merely a terminological one.

**Empirical status:** Neural correlates of self-referential processing are well-studied (default mode network). What happens to DMN activity in advanced meditators during ego-dissolution states is an active research area — Class E (emerging evidence, limited replication).
