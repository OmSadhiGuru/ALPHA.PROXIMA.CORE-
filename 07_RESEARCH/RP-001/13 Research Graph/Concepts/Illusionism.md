# Illusionism

---

## Core Claim

**Phenomenal consciousness as naively conceived — involving irreducible qualia, "what it is like" properties that are intrinsically subjective — does not exist.** The brain generates representations of experience that are mistaken for something metaphysically special. Illusionism "solves" the Hard Problem by dissolving it: there is no Hard Problem because there is no phenomenal consciousness in the relevant sense. [Evidence class: S — DOC-001, p.8]

---

## Key Figures

- **Daniel Dennett** — *Consciousness Explained* (1991); "multiple drafts" model; heterophenomenology; the "Cartesian Theatre" fallacy
- **Keith Frankish** — coined "illusionism" as a label; most explicit contemporary defender

---

## The Illusionist Argument

1. Our introspective reports about consciousness — "there is something it is like to see red" — may be systematically unreliable
2. The brain generates representations of mental states as having phenomenal qualities; these representations are real, but the phenomenal qualities they represent may not exist
3. What we call "consciousness" is the brain's model of its own information processing — a model that simplifies and sometimes distorts the underlying reality
4. The Hard Problem dissolves: we no longer need to explain why physical processes give rise to phenomenal experience, because phenomenal experience as naively conceived is itself a representational construct

---

## Relationship to AST

[[Attention Schema Theory]] (Graziano) is closely aligned with the illusionist tradition: AST proposes that awareness is the brain's simplified model of its own attention, not a direct revelation of some inner phenomenal property. Both positions explain why we *report* consciousness without necessarily explaining why we *have* it (in the phenomenal sense).

---

## The Central Critique

Illusionism faces the **meta-problem of consciousness**: if phenomenal consciousness is an illusion, why does the illusion exist? Why does the brain generate a model of experience as phenomenal in the first place? David Chalmers has argued that the meta-problem — explaining why we think there is a Hard Problem — is itself a version of the Hard Problem.

Critics also argue that illusionism simply relocates the problem: the question "why does this representation seem phenomenally real to the subject?" is still the Hard Problem.

---

## Alpha Proxima Institutional Position

Class S — Illusionism is a philosophically serious position that cannot be dismissed, but it has not achieved empirical traction: it does not generate testable predictions distinct from functionalist theories. It "solves" the Hard Problem by definitional move rather than by empirical investigation.

Alpha Proxima does not commit to illusionism. The Foundation's stance is that phenomenal consciousness is a genuine phenomenon requiring explanation, not dissolution.
