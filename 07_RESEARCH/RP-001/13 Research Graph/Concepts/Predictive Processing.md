# Predictive Processing (PP)

---

## Core Claim

The brain is a **prediction machine**. It continuously generates top-down predictions about sensory inputs and updates these predictions by minimizing prediction errors (the difference between what was predicted and what was received). Conscious experience may arise from the brain's best current prediction about the causes of its sensory inputs. [Evidence class: C for the general PP principle; M for its application to consciousness — DOC-003, p.7]

---

## Mechanism

1. **Generative model:** The brain maintains a hierarchical generative model of the world — a structured set of predictions at multiple levels of abstraction
2. **Top-down prediction:** Higher cortical areas send predictions downward
3. **Prediction error:** Lower areas compute the mismatch between predictions and actual input, sending error signals upward
4. **Belief updating:** The system updates its generative model to minimize prediction error over time
5. **Precision weighting:** Not all prediction errors are equal — attention determines which errors are "precise" (high gain) and therefore influence updates

---

## Relationship to Active Inference

[[Active Inference]] (under Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle) extends PP to action: the agent doesn't only update its model to match the world, it can also *act* to change the world to match its predictions. PP + active inference = a complete perception-action loop. These are often treated as the same theoretical family.

---

## As a Theory of Consciousness

**Critical limitation:** PP is a general computational principle that operates in both conscious and unconscious processing. It requires additional assumptions to explain *why* some predictive processes are conscious and others are not. It is best understood as a **background computational framework** rather than a standalone theory of consciousness. [M — DOC-003, p.7]

Anil Seth's specific application: conscious perception is a "controlled hallucination" — the brain's best prediction about sensory causes, constrained by but not identical to incoming data.

---

## Empirical Support

- Very strong evidence for PP in early sensory processing (visual system, motor control)
- Less direct evidence for PP as specifically explaining consciousness (vs. perception in general) [Emerging evidence — DOC-003, p.7]

---

## AI Implications

PP/Active Inference is **highly tractable for AI** — it can be implemented as a Bayesian inference algorithm. Variational autoencoders, world models, and model-based reinforcement learning all share structural similarities with PP. The Free Energy Principle provides a mathematical framework for designing self-organizing AI systems. [DOC-003, p.7]
