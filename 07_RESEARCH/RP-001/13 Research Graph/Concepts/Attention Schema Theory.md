# Attention Schema Theory (AST)

---

## Core Claim

The brain builds an internal **model of its own attention processes** — an *attention schema* — that it uses to control and predict its own attentional states. Subjective awareness is what it feels like for the brain to have and use this attention schema. Consciousness is not a separate mechanism but the brain's simplified, somewhat inaccurate model of what attention is doing. [Evidence class: M — DOC-003, p.11-12]

---

## Mechanism

1. **Attention:** The brain deploys selective attention — enhancing some signals over others
2. **Attention schema:** The brain builds a simplified model of this attention process (not a perfect model — it omits neurological detail in favor of a usable summary)
3. **Subjective awareness:** The experience of being aware of something *is* the brain's deployment of its attention schema toward that thing
4. **Reports of consciousness:** When asked about subjective experience, the brain reports its attention schema — hence the strong intuition that consciousness is "inner" and "felt"

---

## Relationship to HOT

AST and HOT share the meta-representational intuition: consciousness requires a representation of a representation. AST is more specific — it is about attention modelling, not thought about thought in general. Both theories assign consciousness to a metacognitive, self-representational layer. [DOC-003, p.11-12]

---

## AI Implications

AST is highly tractable for AI. A system with an explicit attention mechanism that also maintains an internal model of its own attention allocation would, under AST, be a candidate for consciousness. This is one of the most actionable theories for LUMIAION-adjacent design. [DOC-003, p.11]

---

## Empirical Support

- Some neuroimaging evidence for attention-related regions mediating awareness reports [Emerging evidence — DOC-003, p.11-12]

---

## Weakness

- The attention schema may explain *reports* of consciousness without explaining *phenomenal* consciousness
- May face the same critique as GNWT: explaining access but not phenomenal experience
