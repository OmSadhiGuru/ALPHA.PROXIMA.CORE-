# Consciousness

---

## Core Definition

Consciousness refers to subjective experience — the quality of there being *something it is like* to be in a particular mental state. This formulation, from philosopher Thomas Nagel, distinguishes consciousness from mere information processing: not all information processing is conscious, and what makes something conscious is the presence of subjective experience.

---

## Key Distinctions

### Access Consciousness vs. Phenomenal Consciousness
- **Access consciousness (A-consciousness):** Information that is globally available to reasoning, reporting, and behavioral control. Can be studied in the third person.
- **Phenomenal consciousness (P-consciousness):** The qualitative, felt character of experience — the "redness" of red, the pain of pain. Cannot be fully captured from the outside.

Most neuroscientific theories (GNWT, HOT, AST) primarily address *access* consciousness. IIT attempts to address *phenomenal* consciousness directly via Φ.

### The Hard Problem
David Chalmers' formulation: even after we fully explain the neural mechanisms of access consciousness, we still face the question of *why* those mechanisms are accompanied by any subjective experience at all. This is the most fundamental unresolved question in consciousness science. [Evidence class: Q — see [[14 Open Questions/RP-001 Open Questions]]]
