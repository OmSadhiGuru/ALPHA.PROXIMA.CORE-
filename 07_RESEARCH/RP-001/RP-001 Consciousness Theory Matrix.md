# RP-001 — Consciousness Theory Matrix

---

## Purpose

This document maps the major theoretical frameworks that attempt to explain consciousness. It does not declare any framework correct. It preserves the genuine intellectual competition between frameworks — a requirement of [[Book III - Knowledge Integrity]], Art. I, Sec. 1.4.

This matrix is the reference point for all RP-001 research notes that touch on theoretical frameworks. When a research note makes a claim about what consciousness "is," it must locate that claim within this matrix.

---

## Classification Note

All major scientific and philosophical frameworks in this matrix are classified **Class M — Competing Models** unless otherwise noted. This means: multiple well-developed frameworks exist that explain the same phenomena but make different predictions or rest on different assumptions. None has definitively displaced the others.

Speculative frameworks (minimal empirical support) are classified **Class S**. Phenomenological frameworks (first-person report traditions) are classified **Class P**, valid within their domain but not generalisable as objective claims without additional evidence.

This classification is mandatory per [[Book III - Knowledge Integrity]], Art. II, and consistent with the current state of consciousness science as of 2026.

## Scientific Frameworks

### Global Workspace Theory (GWT)
**Originator:** Bernard Baars (1988); extended by Dehaene & Changeux  
**Core claim:** Consciousness arises when information is broadcast widely across a "global workspace" — a brain-wide information sharing system. Unconscious processing occurs in specialised modules; consciousness is the integration and broadcast of that processing.  
**Neural prediction:** Correlates with prefrontal-parietal networks; associated with ignition events in EEG/fMRI  
**Strengths:** Strong empirical support; explains access consciousness well; generates testable predictions  
**Limitations:** Better explains *access* consciousness (what we can report) than *phenomenal* consciousness (what it is like); the hard problem remains unanswered  
**Evidence class:** M — Competing Models  
**Primary sources:** Baars (1988) *A Cognitive Theory of Consciousness*; Dehaene et al. (2011) *Experimental and Theoretical Approaches to Conscious Processing*

---

### Integrated Information Theory (IIT)
**Originator:** Giulio Tononi (2004, extended 2008, 2016)  
**Core claim:** Consciousness is identical to integrated information, measured as Φ (phi). Any system with high Φ — that integrates information in a way irreducible to its parts — is conscious, proportional to its Φ value.  
**Distinguishing feature:** Attempts to define consciousness mathematically; implies that consciousness is substrate-independent and potentially present in simple systems  
**Neural prediction:** Consciousness correlates with high Φ in posterior cortical regions; predicts that cerebellum (though more neurons than cortex) is largely unconscious due to low integration  
**Strengths:** Formally rigorous; explains why some brain regions support consciousness more than others; addresses phenomenal consciousness directly  
**Limitations:** Φ is computationally intractable for large systems; some predictions (e.g., feedforward networks are unconscious) are contested; implies simple systems may be conscious, which many find implausible  
**Evidence class:** M — Competing Models  
**Primary sources:** Tononi (2004) *An Information Integration Theory of Consciousness*; Tononi & Koch (2015) *Consciousness: here, there and everywhere?*

---

### Higher-Order Theories (HOT)
**Originator:** David Rosenthal; variants by Ned Block, David Armstrong  
**Core claim:** A mental state is conscious only if there is a higher-order mental state that represents it. Consciousness requires meta-cognition — awareness of one's own mental states.  
**Variants:** Higher-Order Thought theories (HOT); Higher-Order Perception theories; Self-Representationalist theories  
**Strengths:** Explains why some mental states reach consciousness and others don't; connects consciousness to metacognition (well-studied)  
**Limitations:** May confuse access consciousness with phenomenal consciousness; higher-order representation is itself in need of explanation; an infinite regress problem has been raised  
**Evidence class:** M — Competing Models

---

### Predictive Processing / Active Inference
**Originators:** Karl Friston; Andy Clark; Jakob Hohwy  
**Core claim:** The brain is fundamentally a prediction machine. Consciousness is the brain's model of its own predictive processes — specifically, the inferences it makes about the causes of its sensory inputs. Perception is "controlled hallucination."  
**Extension (Active Inference):** Action as well as perception is governed by prediction; the organism acts to fulfil predictions, not just update them  
**Strengths:** Unifies perception, action, and attention under a single framework; explains why consciousness involves a felt sense of reality; strong connections to empirical neuroscience  
**Limitations:** As a general framework, it may be too flexible — difficult to falsify; how exactly Φ, global workspace, or phenomenal character arise from predictive processing remains unclear  
**Evidence class:** M — Competing Models  
**Primary sources:** Friston (2010) *The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?*; Clark (2016) *Surfing Uncertainty*

---

### Recurrent Processing Theory (RPT)
**Originator:** Victor Lamme  
**Core claim:** Consciousness arises from recurrent (feedback) processing in visual cortex and other sensory areas — not from global broadcast. Local recurrent loops are sufficient for phenomenal experience; global workspace is necessary for *reportability* but not for experience itself.  
**Distinguishing feature:** Challenges GWT's claim that consciousness requires global broadcast; argues for a dissociation between phenomenal consciousness and access consciousness  
**Evidence class:** M — Competing Models

## Speculative / Minority Frameworks

### Orchestrated Objective Reduction (Orch-OR)
**Originators:** Roger Penrose and Stuart Hameroff  
**Core claim:** Consciousness involves quantum computations in microtubules within neurons. Objective reduction of quantum superpositions — governed by a quantum gravity theory — generates moments of consciousness.  
**Status:** Highly speculative; no confirmed experimental support for quantum coherence in biological neurons at physiological temperatures; most neuroscientists regard it as implausible with current evidence  
**Evidence class:** S — Speculative Hypotheses  
**Note:** Included because Penrose is a serious mathematician and the framework has philosophical significance even if empirical support is weak

---

### Panpsychism and Panprotopsychism
**Core claim (Panpsychism):** Consciousness or experience is a fundamental feature of reality, present in all matter to some degree. Human consciousness is the complex integration of simpler experiential properties.  
**Core claim (Panprotopsychism):** Reality contains proto-conscious properties that combine to produce consciousness, but simple matter is not itself conscious.  
**Proponents:** David Chalmers (panprotopsychism); Philip Goff (panpsychism); Galen Strawson  
**Status:** Philosophically coherent; empirically unfalsifiable in current form; gains attention as a potential solution to the hard problem that physicalist theories struggle with  
**Evidence class:** S — Speculative Hypotheses for the empirical version; philosophically live as a Class Q for the metaphysical question

## Contemplative and Phenomenological Frameworks

> **Classification note:** The following frameworks are classified Class P (Phenomenological) — first-person accounts of subjective experience, valid within their domain, not generalisable as objective empirical claims without corroborating evidence. They are included in the Atlas because they constitute a significant body of first-person investigation that may inform, and be informed by, empirical frameworks.

### Advaita Vedanta / Non-Dual Framework
**Tradition:** Indian philosophical tradition, Adi Shankaracharya (8th century); contemporary: Ramana Maharshi, Nisargadatta Maharaj  
**Core claim:** Consciousness (Brahman / pure awareness) is the only reality. Individual consciousness (jiva) is not separate from universal consciousness — the apparent separation is Maya (illusion). Direct recognition of non-dual awareness is liberation (Moksha).  
**Phenomenological reports:** Practitioners report states in which the sense of separate self dissolves; a background awareness remains; all phenomena are recognised as arising within awareness rather than outside it.  
**Evidence class:** P — Phenomenological  
**Relationship to empirical science:** Some neural correlates of non-dual awareness states have been studied (experienced meditators in fMRI); results are preliminary (Class E)

---

### Buddhist Phenomenology
**Tradition:** Theravada, Mahayana, Vajrayana; Pali Abhidhamma; contemporary: Mind and Life Institute  
**Core claim:** Consciousness is a stream of momentary arising and passing mental events, not a continuous entity. No permanent self underlies these events (Anatta). Through meditation, the arising and passing of experience can be observed directly.  
**Phenomenological reports:** Practitioners report increasingly refined perception of the momentary nature of experience; cessation states (Nirodha-Samapatti) in which ordinary consciousness temporarily ceases  
**Evidence class:** P — Phenomenological  
**Relationship to empirical science:** Mindfulness research has produced significant empirical findings; these are partially Class C (meditation reduces stress) to Class M (mechanisms are contested) to Class P (experiential reports of advanced states)

---

### Yogic / Tantric Framework
**Tradition:** Hindu Tantra; Kashmir Shaivism; Kundalini yoga traditions  
**Core claim:** Consciousness is layered (Koshas / sheaths); energy (Prana / Shakti) moves through channels (Nadis) and centres (Chakras); advanced practice produces altered states including expansion of consciousness, Samadhi, and Kundalini experiences  
**Relevance to Founder:** The Founder (Om Sadhi Guru) has direct phenomenological experience within this tradition; his reports are classified Class P and will be formally entered into RP-001 as Founder Phenomenological Reports  
**Evidence class:** P — Phenomenological  
**Relationship to empirical science:** Some Kundalini-related phenomena have been studied (altered EEG states, unusual autonomic responses); empirical evidence is Class E (emerging, not yet replicated at scale)

## The Hard Problem

David Chalmers (1995) articulated a distinction that every framework in this matrix must address:

**Easy problems of consciousness:** Explaining the cognitive and neurological functions associated with consciousness — attention, integration, reportability, discrimination. These are "easy" not because they are simple, but because they are, in principle, solvable by the methods of cognitive science and neuroscience.

**Hard problem:** Explaining *why* physical processes are accompanied by subjective experience at all. Why is there *something it is like* to see red? Why doesn't all this neural processing occur "in the dark," without any inner experience?

No framework in this matrix has solved the hard problem. GWT, HOT, and Predictive Processing address the easy problems well. IIT attempts to address the hard problem by making Φ constitutive of experience, but this is contested. Panpsychism dissolves the problem by making experience fundamental. Phenomenological traditions reframe it.

This remains **Class Q — Open Question** at the level of the hard problem itself.
