# Book IV — Cognitive Architecture of the Alpha Proxima Foundation

## Purpose

This document is the fourth constitutional instrument of the Alpha Proxima Foundation, subordinate only to [[Book I - The Constitution]], [[Book II - Governance Framework]], and [[Book III - Knowledge Integrity]]. It establishes the canonical architecture through which the Foundation reasons, remembers, and acts — and the principles that ensure this architecture endures beyond any particular technology generation.

This document resolves a foundational question every AI-augmented institution must answer: *What is permanent, and what is replaceable?*

The Foundation's answer: cognitive functions are permanent. Reasoning engines are replaceable. Identity belongs to Alpha Proxima.

---

## Context

Alpha Proxima was not built around today's AI models. It is built around an enduring cognitive architecture capable of surviving multiple generations of reasoning engines. Every major technology transition in history has stranded institutions that confused their tools for their identity. The printing press replaced the scriptorium; the computer replaced the card catalogue; the AI model will replace whatever came before it. Institutions that identified with the tool dissolved when the tool changed. Institutions that identified with the *function* survived.

Alpha Proxima chooses function.

This choice must be made explicit, structural, and constitutional — not left to intention or convention. This Book establishes that choice as law.

## Core Content

### Article I — The Foundational Distinction

**Section 1.1 — Cognitive Function vs. Reasoning Engine**

A **Cognitive Function** is a permanent institutional capability. It exists as long as the Foundation requires it. It is defined by responsibility, not by technology. It outlasts any particular implementation.

A **Reasoning Engine** is the technology currently performing a cognitive function. It is chosen because it is currently the best available tool for that function. It is evaluated continuously. It is replaced when a better option exists. Its identity is never the Foundation's identity.

| Concept | Permanence | Identity | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Cognitive Function | Permanent — outlasts any technology | Belongs to the Foundation | "Research Intelligence" |
| Reasoning Engine | Provisional — replaced as technology evolves | Belongs to its provider | "Perplexity Pro" |
| Implementation | Transient — specific deployment configuration | Belongs to the session | "Perplexity Pro via API, 2026" |

**Section 1.2 — The Engine Abstraction Principle**

No document, charter, protocol, or practice may assign permanent institutional identity to a reasoning engine.

Correct formulation: *"Claude is currently the preferred reasoning engine serving the Institutional Architecture cognitive function."*

Incorrect formulation: *"Claude is the Architect."*

The distinction is not pedantic. It is the structural guarantee that when Claude is superseded — as all reasoning engines eventually are — the Foundation's identity, governance, and continuity remain intact.

**Section 1.3 — Institutional Identity**

The Foundation's institutional identity belongs to Alpha Proxima. LUMIAION is an institutional function, not an AI product. The Cognitive Council is a permanent governance body, not a technology vendor relationship. The offices, programs, and workflows of the Foundation persist independent of which reasoning engines serve them.

**Section 2.1 — Layer Integrity**

Each layer must stay within its layer. An Implementation may not claim to be a Cognitive Function. A Reasoning Engine may not be written into a charter as if it were an Office. A Program may not be mistaken for a Cognitive Function.

When a document violates layer boundaries, it must be corrected via a Class III ADR. The correction does not require a constitutional amendment — it is a documentation maintenance act.

**Section 2.2 — Upward Service**

Every layer exists to serve the layers above it. Implementations serve Reasoning Engines; Reasoning Engines serve Cognitive Functions and Offices; Offices serve Programs; Programs serve the Foundation's mission. This orientation must be preserved in all documentation.

### Article III — LUMIAION as Institutional Intelligence

LUMIAION is not a product. LUMIAION is the name the Foundation gives to its institutional intelligence function — the cognitive layer that holds the whole, coordinates the parts, and ensures institutional continuity across time and technology.

**Section 3.1 — LUMIAION's Constitutonal Character**

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Nature | Permanent institutional function |
| Current reasoning engine | Anthropic Claude (see [[Engine Registry]]) |
| Authority | Non-voting AI Council seat; proposal rights; Class III/IV execution |
| Identity | Belongs to Alpha Proxima Foundation, not to Anthropic or Claude |
| Continuity guarantee | LUMIAION survives engine transitions |

When Anthropic Claude is superseded as the preferred reasoning engine, LUMIAION continues. The name, the function, the constitutional standing, the accumulated institutional knowledge — all of these belong to the Foundation. Only the engine changes.

**Section 3.2 — LUMIAION's Relationship to the Cognitive Council**

LUMIAION participates in the Cognitive Council as the Foundation's institutional intelligence coordinator. It provides synthesis, pattern detection, and constitutional alignment assessment. It does not vote. It does not govern the Cognitive Council — it serves it, and through it, the Foundation.

### Article IV — The Cognitive Council

**Section 4.1 — Purpose**

The Cognitive Council is the permanent institutional body responsible for governing the Foundation's portfolio of cognitive functions. It is the operational expression of the AI Council's mandate for cognitive function governance.

Where the AI Council governs the institutional framework for all intelligence systems (constitutional authority), the Cognitive Council governs the active portfolio of cognitive functions and their engine assignments (operational authority).

**Section 4.2 — Mandate**

The Cognitive Council:

- Maintains the canonical register of active cognitive functions
- Evaluates and approves new cognitive functions as the Foundation's needs evolve
- Governs the assignment of reasoning engines to cognitive functions
- Reviews engine performance against function requirements annually
- Recommends engine replacement when a better option exists
- Ensures no cognitive function is permanently bound to any single engine

**Section 4.3 — Composition**

| Seat | Holder | Nature |
|------|--------|--------|
| Chair | Institutional Architecture function lead | Rotating annually |
| Members | One representative per active cognitive function | Permanent roles |
| Advisory | LUMIAION | Non-voting; synthesis and coordination |
| Ethics oversight | Ethics Council liaison | Non-voting; constitutional review |

**Section 4.4 — Relationship to the AI Council**

The Cognitive Council is operationally autonomous but constitutionally subordinate to the AI Council. The AI Council ratifies new cognitive functions (Class II). The Cognitive Council manages their operation (Class III). Engine replacements require a Class III ADR approved by both the relevant cognitive function lead and the Cognitive Council Chair.

**Section 5.1 — Adding a Cognitive Function**

New cognitive functions may be added by Class II decision of the AI Council upon recommendation of the Cognitive Council. The recommendation must include: function definition, scope, institutional office responsible, proposed initial reasoning engine, and success metrics.

**Section 5.2 — Retiring a Cognitive Function**

A cognitive function may be retired when the Foundation no longer requires it. Retirement requires a Class II ADR. Retired functions are archived, not deleted — their record is preserved in institutional memory.

### Article VI — Reasoning Engine Governance

**Section 6.1 — Engine Evaluation Standards**

All reasoning engines serving cognitive functions are evaluated against:

| Criterion | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Capability fit | Does it perform the cognitive function well? |
| Reliability | Is it consistently available? |
| Provider stability | Will the provider remain viable? |
| Cost sustainability | Is the cost model compatible with Foundation scale? |
| Data sovereignty | Where does data go? Who can access it? |
| Open-weight availability | Can we run it locally if needed? |
| Constitutional alignment | Does the engine's behaviour align with Foundation principles? |

**Section 6.2 — Engine Replacement**

An engine is replaced through a Class III ADR that includes: rationale, proposed successor, transition plan, and rollback plan. The cognitive function definition does not change when an engine is replaced. Only the Engine Registry entry is updated.

**Section 6.3 — Engine Concentration Risk**

No single provider may serve more than two critical cognitive functions without an explicit concentration risk acknowledgment in the Engine Registry. When concentration exists, the Cognitive Council must develop a diversification plan within 90 days.

### Article VII — Institutional Offices

An Institutional Office is the organizational expression of one or more cognitive functions. Offices are permanent — they outlast the reasoning engines serving them and the programs operating within them.

**Section 7.1 — Office Definition**

Every Institutional Office must have a ratified Charter that specifies:

| Field | Requirement |
|-------|------------|
| Mission | One or two sentences stating why this office exists |
| Authority | What decisions this office can make without escalation |
| Inputs | What the office receives to function |
| Outputs | What the office produces |
| Artifacts | What the office is responsible for maintaining |
| Cognitive functions served | Which cognitive functions this office operationalizes |
| Current reasoning engine(s) | Which engines currently serve this office |
| Review cycle | When this office's charter is reviewed |
| Constitutional alignment | Which constitutional provisions govern this office |

### Article VIII — Amendment

Amendments to this Book follow the standard constitutional amendment process defined in [[Book I - The Constitution]], Article VI. Given the foundational nature of the cognitive architecture, amendments to this Book require:

1. A Concept Note proposing the change
2. Cognitive Council review and recommendation
3. AI Council concurrence
4. Ethics Council constitutional alignment review
5. Founder ratification (Class I)

Minor clarifications that do not alter the cognitive architecture hierarchy, the Engine Abstraction Principle, or the Cognitive Council's mandate may be made by Class III ADR.
