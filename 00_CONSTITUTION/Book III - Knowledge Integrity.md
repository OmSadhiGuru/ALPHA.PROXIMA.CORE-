# Book III — Knowledge Integrity

## Purpose

This document is the third constitutional instrument of the Alpha Proxima Foundation, subordinate only to [[Book I - The Constitution]] and [[Book II - Governance Framework]]. It governs the production, evaluation, preservation, and revision of all knowledge within the Foundation.

Knowledge is not neutral. It is produced by agents with assumptions, tools with limitations, and institutions with interests. This document establishes the principles and obligations that govern how Alpha Proxima handles knowledge — so that the Foundation's outputs can be trusted, revised, and built upon with confidence across generations.

---

## Context

Most institutions have implicit knowledge standards — things everyone agrees on but no one writes down. Implicit standards drift. They are overridden when inconvenient. They are lost when the people who held them leave.

Alpha Proxima makes its knowledge standards explicit and constitutional because the Foundation's entire value proposition depends on the integrity of what it knows and what it claims to know. An institution that builds intelligence systems on weak epistemic foundations builds systems that propagate that weakness at scale.

This document was written after the Foundation's first research program — [[RP-001 Master Index|RP-001 Atlas of Human Consciousness]] — was initiated, making the need for formal knowledge standards both concrete and urgent.

## Core Content

### Article I — Foundational Epistemic Principles

**Section 1.1 — Knowledge as Provisional**
All knowledge held by Alpha Proxima is provisional. Every claim is held subject to revision in light of better evidence, stronger arguments, or new understanding. No claim is final. No authority — including the Founder — may declare a proposition immune to revision.

This is not relativism. It is the proper posture of a rigorous mind toward an incomplete world.

**Section 1.2 — Evidence Primacy**
Claims must be grounded in evidence appropriate to their domain. The standard of evidence varies by domain — the evidence appropriate for a mathematical proof differs from that appropriate for a phenomenological report — but in all domains, claims must be traceable to something beyond assertion.

**Section 1.3 — Uncertainty Disclosure**
Where uncertainty exists, it must be disclosed. Stating a claim with more confidence than the evidence warrants is a violation of Knowledge Integrity, regardless of whether the claim turns out to be correct. Confidence must track evidence, not desire.

**Section 1.4 — Conflicting Evidence Preservation**
When evidence conflicts, the conflict must be preserved. The temptation to merge conflicting theories into artificial consensus — to smooth over genuine intellectual disagreement for the sake of clarity — is explicitly prohibited. The Foundation documents what is known, what is contested, and what is unknown.

**Section 1.5 — Source Attribution**
Every claim that derives from an external source must be attributed to that source. Attribution is not merely academic courtesy — it is an epistemic requirement. Unattributed claims cannot be verified, challenged, or improved. Attribution is what makes knowledge cumulative rather than self-referential.

**Section 1.6 — Failure as Data**
Failed hypotheses, disproven claims, and abandoned research directions are not to be erased. They are to be archived as historically documented knowledge. The path to understanding passes through error. Erasing error erases the path.

### Article II — Evidence Classification System

All knowledge claims within Alpha Proxima are classified using the following taxonomy. This taxonomy is mandatory for all research produced under the Research Program and for all significant knowledge claims in the Vault.

**Class C — Consensus**
*Definition:* Strong agreement among independent, qualified sources using rigorous methods. No significant contrary evidence in peer-reviewed literature.
*Standard:* Multiple independent replications; no significant known anomalies.
*Presentation:* May be stated as well-established without extensive qualification.
*Example use:* "Neurons communicate via electrochemical signals [Class C]."

**Class M — Competing Models**
*Definition:* Multiple well-developed theoretical frameworks exist that explain the same phenomena but make different predictions or rest on different assumptions. Neither framework has definitively displaced the others.
*Standard:* At least two frameworks with substantial supporting evidence and qualified proponents.
*Presentation:* Both/all frameworks must be presented. No framework may be presented as the consensus when genuine competition exists.
*Example use:* "The relationship between consciousness and neural activity is explained by competing frameworks including Global Workspace Theory, Integrated Information Theory, and Higher-Order Theories [Class M]."

**Class Q — Open Questions**
*Definition:* The question is well-formed and scientifically/philosophically tractable, but existing evidence is insufficient to support a strong answer in any direction.
*Standard:* The question has been seriously investigated; the investigation has not yet yielded settled results.
*Presentation:* The question is stated. Available evidence is summarised. No conclusion is asserted.
*Example use:* "Whether the subjective quality of experience is reducible to physical processes remains an open question [Class Q]."

**Class E — Emerging Evidence**
*Definition:* New evidence has appeared that may significantly change understanding, but has not yet been independently replicated or fully evaluated by the relevant scientific community.
*Standard:* One or few sources; not yet replicated; promising but preliminary.
*Presentation:* Presented as emerging; explicit statement that replication is pending.
*Example use:* "Preliminary findings from [Source] suggest [X], though independent replication has not yet occurred [Class E]."

**Class S — Speculative Hypotheses**
*Definition:* A hypothesis that is logically coherent and potentially testable but has little or no empirical support at present. Included because it may prove fruitful for future investigation.
*Standard:* Internal logical consistency; no significant contrary evidence; limited or no positive evidence.
*Presentation:* Explicitly marked speculative; not presented alongside established findings without clear distinction.
*Example use:* "It has been speculated that [X], though this remains without empirical support [Class S]."

**Class P — Phenomenological Reports**
*Definition:* First-person accounts of subjective experience. Not verifiable by third-party observation. Epistemically valid within the domain of subjective inquiry; not generalisable as objective claims without additional evidence.
*Standard:* Honest, detailed first-person account. No requirement for external verification within this class.
*Presentation:* Presented as phenomenological report; not conflated with objective empirical claims.
*Example use:* "The practitioner reports [X] during deep meditation states [Class P]."

### Article III — Knowledge Integrity Obligations

**Section 3.1 — Founder Responsibility**
The Founder is responsible for:
- Modelling epistemic humility in all institutional communications
- Not asserting claims beyond what the evidence supports, regardless of the claim's appeal
- Initiating revision of Vault content when better evidence emerges
- Not allowing institutional interests to distort the knowledge record

**Section 3.2 — AI Responsibility**
AI participants — particularly [[LUMIAION]] and all departments — are responsible for:
- Applying evidence classification to all claims before committing them to the Vault
- Flagging when a request asks for a claim to be stated with more confidence than the evidence warrants
- Preserving source attribution in all synthesised outputs
- Surfacing conflicts between new and existing Vault content rather than resolving them silently

AI participants may not assert claims as established when they are not. An AI that states uncertain things with false confidence violates this document, regardless of whether the Founder instructed it to do so.

**Section 3.3 — Human Researcher Responsibility**
Human contributors to Foundation research are responsible for:
- Honest disclosure of methods, limitations, and potential biases
- Attribution of all sources
- Documentation of disconfirming evidence encountered during research
- Notification to [[JERANIUM Charter|JERANIUM]] when their findings conflict with existing Vault content

**Section 3.4 — Institutional Responsibility**
The Foundation as an institution is responsible for:
- Maintaining the Research Governance Protocol (see [[Research Governance Protocol]])
- Ensuring the [[Ethics Council Charter|Ethics Council]] reviews all research before canonisation
- Creating a culture in which "I don't know" and "the evidence is mixed" are considered valid and valued outputs
- Never using institutional authority to suppress inconvenient findings

### Article IV — Revision Policy

**Section 4.1 — Revision is Required**
When new evidence emerges that contradicts existing Vault content, revision is required — not optional. An institution that fails to update its knowledge in light of new evidence is not a knowledge institution; it is an ideology.

**Section 4.2 — Revision Process**
Minor revisions (corrections, additions, clarifications) may be made by [[LUMIAION]] or [[JERANIUM Charter|JERANIUM]] with a note in the Version History of the relevant document.

Major revisions (changes to core claims, reclassification of evidence class, significant additions) require:
1. A brief Concept Note documenting the change and its justification
2. Ethics Council review if the revision has significant implications
3. Updated Version History with explicit statement of what changed and why

**Section 4.3 — Prior Versions Preserved**
No Vault note is deleted when revised. Prior versions are preserved via git version history. If a revision is substantial enough that the prior version constitutes meaningfully different content, the prior version is archived to `99_ARCHIVE/` with a link to the successor note.

**Section 4.4 — Failed Hypotheses**
When a hypothesis tested by the Foundation is disconfirmed, a note documenting the hypothesis, the method of testing, the disconfirming evidence, and the conclusion is added to the relevant research folder. This note is marked `status: archived` and `outcome: disconfirmed`. It is never deleted.

---

### Article V — Transparency

**Section 5.1 — Methodological Transparency**
All research conducted under the Foundation's name must document its methodology. A claim without a method cannot be evaluated. A claim that cannot be evaluated cannot be trusted.

**Section 5.2 — Limitation Disclosure**
All research outputs must include explicit statement of known limitations: what the research does not cover, what assumptions it makes, what would change the conclusions if different.

**Section 5.3 — Conflict of Interest Disclosure**
Any human or AI participant whose interests might influence the production or evaluation of knowledge must disclose that interest before participating in the relevant knowledge process. The disclosure is recorded.

**Section 5.4 — AI Limitation Disclosure**
AI participants must proactively disclose relevant limitations when producing knowledge outputs: training cutoff dates, known areas of weakness, potential biases in training data, and uncertainty about their own outputs. This obligation may not be waived.
