# Cognitive Council Charter

---

## Purpose

This Charter governs the operations of the Cognitive Council of the Alpha Proxima Foundation. It is a constitutional instrument, derived from and subordinate to [[Book V - Cognitive Council]]. It defines the Council's mission, composition, decision rules, meeting cadence, conflict resolution procedures, relationship governance, and intelligence lifecycle responsibilities.

Where Book V establishes the Cognitive Council as law, this Charter makes it operational.

---

## Mission Statement

The Cognitive Council governs the cognitive function portfolio of the Alpha Proxima Foundation. It ensures that:

1. Every cognitive domain required by the Foundation has a constitutionally defined Cognitive Function
2. Every active Cognitive Function has a currently appointed and continuously evaluated Reasoning Engine
3. The Engine Abstraction Principle is enacted, not merely declared
4. The cognitive function portfolio evolves with the Foundation's mission
5. Intelligence generation across all functions is coordinated, high-quality, and institutionally compounding

The Cognitive Council does not perform cognitive work — it governs the infrastructure through which all cognitive work is performed.

### Quorum

A quorum for Cognitive Council decisions requires the presence of:
- Council Chair
- At least two other Council members
- Founder Representative (for decisions affecting Category A Core Functions)

Decisions without quorum are provisional and subject to retroactive ratification within 7 days.

### Observer Status

Intelligence Office leads not listed above may attend Cognitive Council meetings as non-voting observers. ATHENA, VORTEX, SOHMA, and Community Council representatives hold standing observer status.

## Decision Rules

### Decision Categories

**Category I — Administrative Decisions**
Examples: Meeting scheduling, report format updates, process clarifications, routine registry maintenance.
Rule: Council Chair decides unilaterally, with notification to Council within 24 hours.

**Category II — Operational Decisions**
Examples: Engine performance assessment conclusions, minor scope adjustments, provisional engine suspensions (not replacements), Cognitive Council Directive issuance.
Rule: Simple majority of quorum. Founder Representative notification within 48 hours.

**Category III — Engine Appointment and Replacement Decisions**
Examples: Appointing a new Reasoning Engine, replacing an existing engine, recommending a new Cognitive Function.
Rule: Council consensus (no dissenting votes from present members) + AI Council ratification. Founder Representative must be present or explicitly proxy decision authority. AI Council ratification required within 14 days.

**Category IV — Constitutional Decisions**
Examples: Proposing amendments to Book V, retiring a Cognitive Function, changing the Engine Abstraction Principle.
Rule: Unanimous Cognitive Council vote + AI Council deliberation and ratification + Founder approval. No time constraint — deliberation is required.

### Veto Protocol

The Founder Representative holds unilateral veto over any Category II, III, or IV decision. Veto must be:
- Exercised within 14 days of decision (Category II/III) or 30 days (Category IV)
- Documented with reasoning
- Accompanied by alternative direction or explicit return to Council for re-deliberation

If no veto is exercised within the stated window, the decision is implicitly ratified by the Founder.

### Dissent Recording

Any Council member may formally record dissent on any decision. Recorded dissents are:
- Included in the Cognitive Council meeting record
- Reviewed at the next quarterly review
- Considered when outcomes are later evaluated

Dissent is not obstruction — it is institutional memory.

## Responsibilities

### 1. Cognitive Function Portfolio Governance

The Cognitive Council maintains the complete map of the Foundation's cognitive function requirements and ensures every requirement has a constitutionally defined function to address it.

**Portfolio review includes:**
- Are there cognitive domains the Foundation needs that lack a defined function?
- Are there defined functions that the Foundation no longer needs?
- Are function categories (A/B/C) still accurate?
- Are function definitions still current with the Foundation's evolved mission?

### 2. Engine Appointment and Evaluation

For each active Cognitive Function, the Cognitive Council:
- Maintains a current engine appointment
- Continuously monitors engine performance against the 10 criteria of the Engine Succession Policy
- Initiates Engine Evaluation processes when performance concerns arise
- Manages engine transitions when replacements are warranted

### 3. Intelligence Lifecycle Governance

The Cognitive Council governs the full intelligence lifecycle:

```
Cognitive Function Activation
          ↓
Engine Appointment
          ↓
Operational Monitoring (continuous)
          ↓
Performance Evaluation (per Engine Succession Policy)
          ↓
    ┌─────────────────────┐
    │ Performance OK      │ → Continue appointment
    └─────────────────────┘
    ┌─────────────────────┐
    │ Performance Concern  │ → Enhanced monitoring period
    └─────────────────────┘
    ┌─────────────────────┐
    │ Succession Required │ → Engine Succession Process
    └─────────────────────┘
          ↓
Engine Transition
          ↓
New Engine Appointment
```

### 4. Cognitive Council Directive Issuance

The Cognitive Council may issue CC-series directives governing cognitive function operations. CC-series directives:
- Are operational (not constitutional)
- Apply across functions or to specific functions
- Are recorded in the Cognitive Council Directive Register
- Expire when superseded by a newer directive or when the Cognitive Council explicitly retires them

### 5. Cross-Function Coordination

The Cognitive Council manages cross-function coordination requirements, including:
- Research program function assignments (which functions are engaged per commission)
- Independence protocol enforcement
- Stage gate definitions for multi-function programs
- Conflict resolution when functions produce contradictory outputs

### 6. Constitutional Function Registry Maintenance

The Cognitive Council, through LUMIAION as maintainer, keeps the [[Cognitive Function Registry]] current:
- Engine appointment records updated with each change
- Function status (Active/Standby/Suspended) maintained
- Function category reviews conducted annually
- Registry amendments require Cognitive Council approval and AI Council ratification for constitutional elements

## Conflict Resolution

### Type 1 — Contradictory Function Outputs

When two or more Cognitive Functions produce contradictory outputs on the same question:

1. **Documentation:** The contradiction is formally documented, with both outputs preserved. Neither is discarded.
2. **Domain Analysis:** The Cognitive Council determines whose domain the question properly belongs to.
3. **Evidence Hierarchy:** If the contradiction is empirical, the evidence strength hierarchy (C > M > E > Q > S > P) applies.
4. **Escalation:** If the contradiction cannot be resolved through domain analysis or evidence hierarchy, it is escalated to the Research Intelligence Office for adjudication, with Ethics Intelligence review if ethical dimensions are present.
5. **Unresolved Contradiction:** If genuinely unresolvable, both positions are preserved in the canonical output with explicit notation that the question remains contested.

False consensus is worse than acknowledged contradiction. The Cognitive Council never resolves contradiction by suppression.

### Type 2 — Engine Appointment Disputes

When Council members disagree on engine appointment or replacement:

1. A formal Engine Evaluation is conducted using all 10 criteria of the Engine Succession Policy.
2. Evaluation results are presented to the full Council.
3. Category III decision rules apply (consensus required).
4. If consensus is not achievable, the Founder Representative makes the final determination.

### Type 3 — Function Scope Disputes

When two functions claim domain authority over the same task:

1. The Cognitive Council convenes a scope arbitration.
2. Primary consideration: which function's mission statement most directly addresses the task?
3. If still ambiguous: what does the research program commission specify?
4. Resolution is recorded as a precedent for future similar disputes.

### Type 4 — Constitutional Interpretation Disputes

When the meaning of Book V or this Charter is disputed:

1. The Cognitive Council produces written interpretations from each disagreeing perspective.
2. The matter is escalated to the AI Council for constitutional interpretation.
3. The AI Council's interpretation is binding.
4. If the dispute reveals a genuine gap in the constitutional text, an amendment process is initiated.

## Cross-Office Collaboration

### With the AI Council

The AI Council holds ratification authority over all Category III and IV Cognitive Council decisions. The collaboration protocol:

1. Cognitive Council passes a Category III/IV decision
2. Cognitive Council Chair formally requests AI Council ratification, providing full decision record and supporting analysis
3. AI Council deliberates within 14 days (Category III) or at their discretion (Category IV)
4. AI Council ratification is recorded in both the Cognitive Council record and the AI Council Registry
5. If AI Council declines ratification, the matter returns to Cognitive Council for revision

### With the Executive Office

The Executive Office provides strategic direction to the Cognitive Council through:
- Strategic priority signals (which cognitive domains to prioritize)
- Resource allocation decisions
- Founder Directive execution (Founder Directives may mandate cognitive architecture changes)

The Cognitive Council provides the Executive Office:
- Cognitive function portfolio status
- Engine performance intelligence
- Capability gap identification
- Cognitive function roadmap recommendations

### With Intelligence Offices

Intelligence Offices (the operational performers of Cognitive Functions) receive from the Cognitive Council:
- Engine appointment decisions
- Performance standards and evaluation criteria
- Scope definitions and function boundaries
- Cross-function coordination protocols

Intelligence Offices provide to the Cognitive Council:
- Performance self-assessments
- Engine performance concerns (early warning)
- Scope expansion requests
- Engine capability insights

## Engine Succession Triggers

The following conditions trigger an Engine Succession Process:

| Trigger | Classification | Timeline |
|---------|---------------|---------|
| Engine provider discontinues service | Emergency | Immediate |
| Engine performance falls below threshold on 3+ criteria | Standard | 30 days |
| Superior engine identified by Observatory or external source | Standard | 60 days |
| Founder directive to change engine | Mandatory | As specified |
| Ethics violation by engine provider | Emergency | Immediate |
| Cost exceeds sustainable level without performance justification | Standard | 30 days |
| New cognitive function requires engine appointment | Standard | 14 days |

3. **AI Council liaison:** Formal escalations and ratification requests
4. **Directive register management:** CC-series directives tracked and maintained
5. **Cross-function coordination:** Ensuring independence protocols are honored; stage gate management
6. **Escalation management:** Knowing when to escalate to Founder vs. resolve operationally
7. **Annual report:** Producing the Annual Cognitive Function Portfolio Report for Founder and AI Council

## Annual Cognitive Function Portfolio Report

Each year, the Council Chair produces the Annual Cognitive Function Portfolio Report, covering:

1. **Portfolio Status:** Every function's current status, engine appointment, and performance assessment
2. **Engine Transitions:** All engine changes made during the year and their outcomes
3. **New Functions:** Any new functions added; any functions retired or suspended
4. **Gaps Identified:** Cognitive domains with no current function; functions without adequate engines
5. **Strategic Recommendations:** What the portfolio needs in the coming year to serve the Foundation's mission
6. **Constitutional Health:** Assessment of whether Book V and this Charter remain fit for purpose
